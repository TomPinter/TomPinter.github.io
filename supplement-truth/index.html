I've Taken Supplements Every Day for 50 Years.
Here's What I Actually Learned.

By Tom Pinter  ·  June 13, 2026  ·  10 min read

When I was a child, I was often ill. Throat infections, then pneumonia, then rubella. Doctors wanted to remove my tonsils.

My mother had a different idea.

She noticed that during every illness, doctors prescribed vitamins anyway. Her reasoning: if vitamins help during illness, why not take them consistently to prevent the illness in the first place?

She was right.

With daily supplementation, the infections stopped completely. Even during major epidemics, I didn't catch anything. That was the beginning of something I've maintained for over 50 years — and the clearest demonstration I know that this isn't a theory.

But most people who try supplements don't get that result. They take something for a few weeks, feel nothing, and quit. So what's the difference?

The wrong question

Most people start with the wrong question: "Are supplements worth it?"

That question leads nowhere useful. Some people feel immediate effects. Others notice nothing for weeks. Some don't notice much at all — until they stop and feel the absence.

The right question is: What nutrients does my body actually need every day, and am I consistently providing them?

That shift changes everything.

Why food alone is no longer enough

The assumption most people make is that a reasonably healthy diet covers the basics. It used to be closer to true. Today, it isn't.

Modern agriculture relies on NPK fertilizer — nitrogen, phosphorus, and potassium. These three nutrients maximize crop yield. But the soil naturally contained roughly 60 minerals, not three. The others are simply not replaced. Crops grow bigger, but they're nutritionally thinner.

The mechanism runs deeper than the fertilizers themselves. Soil bacteria normally pre-digest minerals for plants — breaking them down into a form the plant can absorb. Herbicides and pesticides kill those bacteria. Without them, even whatever minerals remain in the soil don't fully enter the plant.

There's a second factor that compounds this: environmental pollution. We're exposed to far more toxic load than previous generations — from air, water, food packaging, and countless other sources. Neutralizing that load requires nutrients. Which means the daily requirement isn't just staying level with what previous generations needed — it's higher.

I noticed this directly. When I ate average-quality food and skipped supplements for just a few days — not junk food, not processed products, but normal home-cooked meals from store-bought ingredients — I developed clear deficiency symptoms. Not dramatic ones, but noticeable enough to be unambiguous.

Food has not become poison. But relying on it alone to cover all nutritional requirements is no longer a reliable strategy.

What "essential" actually means

The word "essential" has a specific meaning in nutrition. It does not mean important or beneficial. It means: the body cannot produce it. If you don't consume it, you don't have it.

The full list of essential nutrients the body needs every day adds up to roughly 90:

  • 60 minerals
  • 16 vitamins
  • 12 essential amino acids
  • 3 essential fatty acids

Each one is required for one or more specific biological functions. When a function cannot run for lack of its required nutrient, something fails. The failure may be silent for years — or it may show up immediately. Either way, it accumulates.

A physician who spent decades studying nutrient deficiencies — examining the records of more than 17,500 autopsies — concluded that every natural death traces to one or more nutritional deficiencies. That's a striking conclusion. But it's consistent with what I've observed over 50 years: deficiency is always the greater risk. "Overdose" on essential nutrients is nearly impossible when ratios are maintained. Deficiency is a certainty if you're not actively preventing it.

A starting framework for adults

There's a basic combination that covers the daily essential range for a healthy adult. I'll share it here as a starting framework — not as a prescription, and not as my personal current stack.

The right amounts vary significantly by body size, lifestyle, health history, and individual biochemistry. The starting point:

  • Vitamin C — 1,000 mg
  • B-50 complex + 250 mg additional B1
  • Vitamin A — 10,000 IU
  • Vitamin D — 400–4,000 IU
  • Vitamin E — 400 IU
  • Mineral complex — including calcium 500 mg, magnesium 250 mg, zinc, iron, and others
  • Kelp trace element tablet

Vitamin C and the calcium–magnesium combination are the two that vary most between individuals. The only reliable way to find your right amount is to test it — more on that in a moment.

If you have specific health concerns or a demanding lifestyle, this baseline is the floor, not the ceiling.

Why single supplements rarely work

This is the piece most people miss — and it explains the "I took it for a month and felt nothing" experience.

Nutrients do not work like medications.

A medication targets a specific receptor or pathway. A nutrient is a raw material for thousands of simultaneous chemical processes. Focusing on one nutrient while neglecting the others doesn't necessarily improve the system — and it can make things worse, because you've shifted the ratio without addressing the underlying shortfall.

Think of it as a mechanism with many gears. If one gear is wrong, the system fails. Adding more of the same gear while the others remain broken doesn't necessarily fix the mechanism. You need the full set, in the right proportions, working together.

That's why providing the complete range consistently produces results that individual supplements rarely match. The goal is not to target one symptom. It's to give the entire system what it needs to run correctly.

Why overdosing is not the real concern

Most people who are cautious about supplements worry about overdosing. In my experience, this concern is nearly always misdirected.

The body selects what it needs and eliminates the rest. This is how essential nutrients work by definition. The body has mechanisms for excreting excess — and it uses them. What it cannot do is conjure essential nutrients from nothing.

The real risk runs the other direction: deficiency.

In 50 years, I've never encountered a case where someone was harmed by consistent intake of essential nutrients at sensible levels. I've seen many cases where deficiency produced ongoing, unexplained symptoms that resolved once the nutritional gap was filled.

The economics are straightforward. Daily supplementation costs roughly what a cup of coffee costs. The cost of deficiency doesn't show up on a price tag until much later — and by then it's harder to address.

What to expect when you start

Most people experience nothing unusual when they begin supplementing. But some people — particularly those with a history of alcohol use, illegal drug or narcotic use, or significant chemical or pharmaceutical substance exposure — may notice temporary effects.

Here's why: certain substances can accumulate in cells over years. When nutrition improves and the body's detoxification processes activate, those stored substances can temporarily enter the bloodstream before being eliminated. Whatever effect those substances are capable of producing, they may produce briefly during that transit.

Someone with a history of heavy alcohol use, for example, might feel a faint, passing intoxicated sensation as stored alcohol residues mobilize. Someone who used illegal drugs or narcotics may notice something unfamiliar as those residues move. Similar patterns can occur with significant chemical or pharmaceutical substance exposures.

This is a recognized phenomenon in naturopathic medicine: the Herxheimer reaction, sometimes called a healing crisis or die-off reaction. It is typically mild and temporary. The key message: do not panic. If something like this happens, it's a sign that substances are moving out.

There are also straightforward calibration signals worth knowing before you start:

B vitamins make urine appear bright yellow. This is normal. It's not a warning.

Vitamin C can cause loose stools when you've taken more than your body currently requires. This is a calibration signal, not a side effect to fear. If it happens, reduce the dose and work back up gradually until you find your level.

Magnesium in excess can cause bloating or loose stools. Same principle: reduce and calibrate.

Staying well hydrated supports the elimination process. Rest when needed.

What actually matters when choosing brands

Price is not the signal most people think it is.

Across 50 years of supplementing — in different countries, at different price points, from different manufacturers — my experience is consistent: if the right nutrients are present in the right amounts, the results are the same regardless of what the label costs.

What does matter is the source.

Established supplement brands sold through established supplement retailers have track records. Other customers' experiences are visible and verifiable. Quality is predictable.

A completely new company, a pharmaceutical-industry supplement product, or anything marketed primarily through aggressive scientific-sounding claims is a different category. Convincing promotion and polished branding do not guarantee quality — and this is especially true for products that appear suddenly with dramatic claims.

The practical filter: choose companies and retailers that have been around long enough to have real user histories behind their products.

Herbs are a different category

One thing worth clarifying before you go: herbs and essential nutrients are not substitutes for each other.

Essential nutrients are the foundation — the raw materials every cell in your body needs to function. There's no alternative to providing them.

Herbs are tools. They can support specific functions or address specific conditions — and they can complement a solid nutritional foundation effectively. But they cannot replace what essential nutrients provide. A specialized tool does not substitute for structural materials.

The practical order is clear: build the foundation first. Once essential nutrition is consistent, targeted supplements like herbs add value on top of it.
